Excellence

Do Japanese and Germans make better engineers?

We are all familiar with the idea of Japanese and German engineering. It is no mistake of history that many modern vehicle brands have their origin in one of these two countries. German rocket science is part of the reason humans were able to go to the moon. The Japanese have helped push the proliferation of the semi-conductor in the modern world. These types of projects are immensely unforgiving. A wrongly placed transistor will invalidate an entire chip design. A poorly placed valve will mean the difference between space-flight and death for astronauts. Why have only a limited number of nations proven themselves to be capable of reliably manufacturing complex products at extremely high qualities?

I believe this is not a question of race, but of culture. In the same way individuals can be disposed to certain personality traits, a society can collectively imbue certain behavioural properties. One of these properties I will call "excellence". This term is used in many ways, but here I will use it to describe the combined industriousness and conscientious that is characteristic of high-functioning nations and individuals. Discipline is necessary, but not sufficient to obtain excellence. Excellence is rigour without dogma. It is passion combined with reason.

Different societies throughout history have exhibited this property to a greater and lesser extent. Civilizations like those of ancient Egypt, China, Arabia and Greece shone like bright beacons compared to their neighbors (to whom they generally refered to as "barbarians").

Excellence in a society is often correlated with a strong intellectual tradition. Before the world wars of the 20th century, Germany had one of the strongest and deepest intellectual cultures the world has ever seen. Why has Germany produced a disproportionate number of the greatest western composers, mathematicians, physicists and philosophers? This is also true of the thinkers of Russia, regardless of your thoughts of their politics. People with limited resources were able to apply themselves, and achieve such great mental clarity that their works are often still the furthest foray in those fields (eg. general relativity, fugues of Bach, Gödel's incompletenes theorem). Understanding why these societies oriented themselves in the way they did would require psychological and societal analysis that are beyond my abilities. I do suspect, however, that this is a result of the emphasis of personal agency and celebrating intellectualism.

Excellence appears to be relatively low (and decreasing) in the current age. Since the enlightenment, the story of the western world has been one of regression. Language becomes less elaborate, clothing becomes less formal, music becomes more repetitive. Nothing is too graphic to put on TV or YouTube. If culture is progressing, should we not become more sensitive to violence rather than desensitised to it? We tend to think of previous generations as being prudish, but what is prudishness except for high self-standards? The trajectory of our culture seems to be towards the cave-man rather than the enlightened individual. Indeed the world of the prehistoric man was maximumally explicit, since there was no concept of clothing, and no way to contain violence.

Modern art is often deliberately ugly or unintellectual to "subvert expectations". Most people intuitively sense that modern art is nonsense, but are told off by the art "experts" as not having true taste. I believe that this is a case of the emperors new clothes. My assumption is that modern artists have looked at the great masters, determined they will never be as skilled and now instead fight over who can make the most idiotic, lazy art imaginable. Modern architecture is completely devoid of humanity, and crumbles within 15 years. Meanwhile structures that are hundreds or thousands of years old continue to stand, and often capture something truly transcendent about the human experience. We have become disinterested in asking the big questions. Philosophers have determined that everything is subjective and there is no such thing as objective good.

I believe that excellence is the answer to nihilism. If our lives are finite, and all our actions in the world will eventually be forgotten, then what is the point of doing anything if not for the highest possible virtue? Dedicating yourself to excellence is basically a middle-finger to entropy. That is why I believe it is important to strive beyond the call of duty.

Existence does not make sense otherwise.
